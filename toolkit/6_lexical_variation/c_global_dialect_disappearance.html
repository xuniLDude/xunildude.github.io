Globalis - Dialect levelling and Variety

Will dialects and local varieties disappear?

A dialect is a language variety characteristic of a particular group of speakers. It has particular differences in grammar and vocabulary that distinguishes it from other. A dialect may come with an accent, a particular way to pronounce words.

Dialect and accent do not necessarily bond strongly. A person with an Irish accent can read and use Standard English and it not limited to using Irish English.

Standard British English spread around the world and it now not geographically limited to England.

Different countries have their own Standard English: Standard American English, Standard Canadian English, Standard Australian English, ...

Levelling

Differences in dialects level out as mobility increases. Children are exposed to many dialects, at school, social settings, online, telecommunications. Language changes and it passed on to their own children. The use of local dialects diminishes and the use of Standard English increases.

But, language (dialect) is not passed on perfectly to next generations. Every generation speaks a little differently. Also, lexis is expanded and reduced, subject to development. Young people use language for indentity purposes. New lexis is needed: netbook, alcopop. New loan words are integrated: yoghurt, tapas, ...

If a particular identity is built, it must deviate from Standard English.

Varieties and Identity

As mentioned, exposure to Standard English (media, school, online) can reduce differences in dialects. There is pressure to standardize English around the worls, often for economic reasons.

Will local varieties disappear? Reasons that speak against it:
  • Exposure to outside influences vary in households and communities
  • Not everyone is exposed to the same experiences, level of globalisation
  • The need to maintain a sense of identity, pride of one's own origins

In many situtaions it is more appropriate to use local dialects over the Standard one.
