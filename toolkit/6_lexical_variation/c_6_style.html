Style

Differences between synonyms can be one of style. Formal and informal settings both use require different styles.

Style is learned through language use and observation, and recognise which words are appropriate.

Informal vs formal

Colloquial speech is casual language spoken or written: tweet, notes, call.

Formal lexis is longer than informal; often of Latin origin: get/obtain, so/consequently, crash/collision.

Informal language often referred to Anglo-Saxon (Old English) due to its coarseness. Much of everyday language (including swear-words).

My father can drive me to the station*.

The only words not derived from Old English is station (French-Latin origin).

Informal English often leaves out the details. By it is it understood that the phone is meant. If people around use understand what we mean to say, there is no need to spell things out (be precise).

Jargon

Jargon is specialised lexis, applicable to specific subject domains. Language can make the explaining of certain things uncesserality complicated. Jargon simplifies this process. Often everyday language cannot explain complicated concepts and processes. Jargon often makes use of hyponyms.

Jargon is not limited to professions, it is unique to a community in need to having precise definitions. Skateboarding is not an academic profession, but ollies, nollies, hardflips are jargon appearing in this domain.
