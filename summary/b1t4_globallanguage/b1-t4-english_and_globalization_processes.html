Globalization - Processes

Instead of looking at the consequences of Globalisation, look at the what leads to Globalisation.

Important: English is a facilitator of Globalisation, and Globalisation in turn affects the English language.

Definition and processes

Oxford defines Globalisation as (Oxford dict.) The process by which businesses or other organizations develop international influence or start operating on an international scale.

Extrinsic reasons are responsible for Globalisation (not instrinsic ones) and are social processes that change society.

Various processes are involved in Globalisation:

  • Social processes
  • Econonomical processes
  • Political processes

Interconnected system

Complex social, political, and economical processes turn the world into a interconneced system.

The role of English

  • Globalisation affects English, English is a tool for globalisation. Bi-directional relationship.
  • English has become the 'Lingua Franca' of Globalisation
  • English is actively promoted in education, language policies. In this respect English is a facilitator of Globalization!

Emergence of new varieties

  • Contact with local languages affects English
  • English words can have different meanings in different communities (e.g. mouton, meat or animal?)
  • Multiplex: as a global language English becomes multiplex - it has no more centre (example: the UK). It has its centre in different communities that use it.
  • New Englishes emerge, tailor-made to the needs of communities (e.g. online community)

Time, space, mobility

  • Time and space restricted interconnectedness; Global communciation systems, Internet, Video conferencing break down such limitations. No matter where one is, connectivity is omnipresent.
  • Distance is no longer a matter, video conferencing removes the need to travel.
  • Services can be rendered from distances far away from one's own location: e.g. call centres

Result: Change in social structures

Change in social structures

New global communities emerge, members from all over the world (in English). Specific interests groups emerge, with global members (in English).

Face-to-face contact may no longer be of importance in some communities.

New cultural identities emerge based on Global communities, different from local identities
