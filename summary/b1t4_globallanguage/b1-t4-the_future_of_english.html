The future and patterns

How does English develop and what patterns can be seen?

General outcomes

  • Number of native English speakers will fall
  • Possibility for a linguistic fashion to be started by a non-native language speaking group which will be adopted on a larger scale (example: rapping)
  • Concord rules: (three person i.o. three people) may get accepted
  • Countability rules: furinitures i.o. furniture, kitchenware i.o. kitchenwares

Social, Political, Sociolinguistic outcomes

  • What power and prestige is associated with new varieties of Englishes?
  • In the press, words are no longer put in inverted commas, they are used less self-consciously. Slowly adopted by native speakers.
  • Loan words: New Zealand: Maori words mixed into English. Politicians, leaders, social groups start to adopt and accept loan words and promote the use.
  • Political turf: Regional English varieties are used for indentity purposes (internationally or at home). Particular language use can give a person (politician) a unique identity. Incorrect language use may no longer be frowned upon, but accepted as a way of identification.
  • Code-mixing: Other languages are mixed with English. First, condmned because it does not adhere to the rules of an underlying language, then at some point accepted. (example: Singlish; hybrid of English, Chinese, Malay) used in Singapore.

Mutual intelligibility vs. local identity

  • In England, standard English is promoted for mutual intelligibility. At the same time, maintaining the value of local dialects and accents.
  • Strong native presences build in countries where English is not the first language. Just how much deviation from standard English is acceptable?
  • If people using varieties for indentity purposes become influential, usages will become more acceptable.
  • Instead of supplementary vocabulary (words not used in other countries, but that do not exist in standard English, e.g. flora, fauna), there will be local alternatives instead.

What is English now?

  • It is NOT a cohesive and homogenous language as it has been transforming.
  • When we say "they speak English", it does not mean they understand each other; they may be speaking different languages.

Hybrids

Code-switching between English and another language. Creating problems understanding each other.

Translation

When is translation necessary even if both parties speak (an) English. Will translation between Englishes become a reality?

Two forces splitting English (and Globalisation)

Need for Lingua Franca:

The need for a 'Lingua France' as a facilitator for globalisation.

Diversification:

  • English adapts to the need of communities that use it. It diversifies.
  • It is wrong to say that Globalisation leads to linguistic homogeneity (everyone speaking the same language)
  • To a certain extent, economically and politically strong nations (UK, USA) do exert linguistic power on weaker nations, esepcially in scientific reasearch and academic writing.
  • Linguistic globalisation is a consequence of 'mobility' and 'interconnectedness'. Communities adapt local cultures, global communication affects them.
  • English is a facilitator of Globalisation, and Globalisation in turn affects the English language. New varieties of English emerge.
