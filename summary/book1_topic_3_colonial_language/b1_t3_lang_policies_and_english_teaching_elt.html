Language policies and English teaching (ELT)

English teaching and language policies during colonial times greatly have greatly influenced today's English language teaching approaches.

Colonial language policy between 4 poles

Pole Description
Pole 1 Produce docile and compliant workers
Pole 2 Satisfy European need to bring civilization to the world through English.
Pole 3 Constigencies of class, ethnicity, race and economic development.
Pole 4 'Orientalism': insistence on exotic histories, traditions, and nations in decline.

English vs local language education

Juggling between the four poles, the general decision leaned toward local language education.

Implications during and after colonialism on ELT

  • English widely regarded as superior
  • English mostly taught to the elite
  • Despite the limited role of ELT (English language teaching) withing the British Empire, the global position of English greatly influenced the development of ELT.
  • Plenty of thought about English and English teaching have their origins in colonial contexts rather than in Britain itself.
  • ELT theories and practices developed in the 'Empire' (in colonies) were imported back to into Britain.
  • Despite the fact that Europeans tend to write colonialism out of historical development, experience with ELT must be attributed to the colonial experience.
