Substrate, Pidgin, Creole

A collection of interesting aspects and terms in the development of varieties.

Feature Description Territories
Substrate Relatively small political and economical control imposed on colony.
  • Community became bilingual
  • Intimate contact between English and local language
  • Strong influence on English: Radically divergent forms of English arose
  • Local language features incorporated into English. In this case local language is called a Substrate.
  • West Africa
  • India
Substrate Hiberno-English (Irish English)

Example:

'They are after doing the work' vs. 'They have just done the work'

'after' AND '-ing' indicate immediate past
  • Ireland
Pidgin Pidgin:

Lexis from English, Grammar derived elsewhere.

Developed as a means for communication between communities that had no common language.

Does NOT have native speakers.

Learned by people who have a first language.

Small lexis and little grammatical complexity.

English-based pidgin: Lexis taken from a lexifier language (English)

Expanded Pidgin: versatile pidgin with applications in various domains, but does not have status of a creole (is not first language of learners).

 American colonies with African slaves
Creole Pidgin → Creole: called Creolisation

If a pidgin is the first language a person learns, it is a creole.

Creolisation extends a pidgin to make it suitable for a wider range of applications.
