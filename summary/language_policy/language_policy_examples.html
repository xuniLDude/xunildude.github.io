Language policy examples

Pakistan and Tanzania are two former British colonies that underwent language planning.

Pakistan

Attention: Policy goal: a Muslim language as the official language. English seens as another form of colonisation.
  • Gained independence in 1947
  • Country was divided into two self-governing dominions (Pakistan, India)
  • Pakistan split into two teritories in 1971: East and West
  • English taught in educational setting during colonial times, widely known
  • English was not part of national and religious moevements; kept a neutral position
  • English favoured as official language, but idea strongly opposed by religious groups. Reason: Keeping English is a new form of colisation. A Muslim language was needed.
  • Urdu chosen as the official language, country underwent Urduisation. But elite schools kept English as the medium and demand for English education grew, even among government officials.
  • Urdu was abandoned, English reinstated as the official language. Reasons: local demand (pressure from society), giving the country a global position in regards to economics and politic.
  • Urdu is (2012) the national language, English and Urdu have official status.
  • Conclusion: Policy implemented in a hurry and failed, not considering factors that lead to abandonment. English remained a tool for the elite: ensures social distinction from non-elite. English is a facilitator for a social class system.

Tanzania

Attention: Policy goal: Turn away from Western Capitalist models, and build a class-less society based on socialism and self-reliance(!).
  • Gained independence in 1961
  • English firmly established language already (in country)
  • From 1967 to 1974 strong antagonism towards Britain and other capitalist countries. Swahili strongly promoted o replace English. Effort was put into acquisition and corpus planning. Full Swahilisation never came about.
  • Country realized due to political events and economic pressures that it could not be self-reliant and isolated from the West.
  • Economic liberalisation in 1967, efforts made to equalize status of English and Swahili. English gained again momentum as during colonial periods.
  • Both languages remain strong and taught at school. Swahili identifies the locals. New language forms emerge, mixing English with Swahili.
