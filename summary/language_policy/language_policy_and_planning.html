Language policy planning and implementation

Language policy is a set of broad goals.

Language policy

A language policy regulates the implementation of an official language (as opposed to a national language) and is the umbrella term for:

  • Rules (how language shall be used)
  • Language planning (implementation of rules)
  • Status planning (how to implement the rules)
  • Acquisition planning (how language shall be acquired)
  • Corpus planning (including expansion of language functions, lexis)

Who decides over a language policy?

New policies are generally determined by politicians based on their vision of what they country needs and how the country shall position itselfs its region and the world.

Legislation of a country regulates which languages are to be used for official purposes in government at all levels (national, regional, and local), in the civil service, educational institutions, legal domain, and as a means of correspondence with other countries.

Selection criteria:

  • Large corpus (English is highly suitable) [ Pragmatic decision ]
  • Politics
  • Shall the local community be disadvantaged by a language the elite speaks?
  • Stands towards the west
  • Local culture
  • History (a colonial language leaves behind memories)
  • Identification and positioning of country in the region and world
  • Religion and possible side-effects

Points to consider in the selection process:

  • Where does the country want to be position itself, regionally and internationally? Language heavily contributes to success and failure.
  • Ideology vs pragmatism: what language is more practical and easier to implement. Ideological views may get in the way.
  • Weigh advantages and disadvantages of the status quo in regard to official languages: how widely known is a language among society(!!!) ?
  • If an indigenous langage is promoted to official, is its corpus sufficient? If not, how will it be extended, and in what time frame.
  • English for example, allows access to the international market and knowledge banks.
  • English may be an ethnically neutral language (no political strings attached) and serve as a tool to unify a country, as opposed to forming a divide (hard feelings against one language).
  • English has abundant teaching materials that could be deployed quickly. An indigenous language may not have them.
  • Will all members of the government understand the new official language? Will translating be necessary?
Important: Faults in the decision making process often appear when the policy is implemented, and often deemed unachievable.
Important: In many former British colonies, English was retained as an official language. In other cases, such as Pakistan and Tanzania, English as an official medium was contested.

Although the English corpus is large, it does not automatically mean it becomes the official language.

Definitions

The below table explains termnology involved in language policy.

Term Description
Language policy

Umbrella term

Language policy is a set of board goals -political, social, economic, linguistic- that policy developers hope to achieve by focusing on aspects of the use of languages in particular countries.
Language planning Identifies processes of policy implementation
Official language Language used for official purposes in government at all levels (national, regional, and local), in the civil service, educational institutions, legal domain, and as a means of correspondence with other countries.

Official languages have high status, and may apply to the entire country, or only to certain regions.

A country may have more than one official language.
National language A national language has symbolic value.

Often the language that locals identify with; the identity of the people. They may not feel the same about the official language.
Status planning The process of planning how to give effect to decisions of language policy.
Acquisition planning Planning of how people will learn the new official language and other languages the government deems important.

Acquisition is one of the most crucial aspects. How will students learn the language at school? Acquisition planning involves teachers, linguists, developers of educational materials, etc.
Corpus planning Corpus planning involves linguists and educators. The corpus of a (new) official language may need to be expanded to be able to serve all necessary language functions (example: teaching mathematics).
Policy reformulation Identified policy goals are not always met. Noticing deviations does not automatically lead to reformulation as implementation is slow and expensive. A policy may stay in place until there is string political or economic pressure to update or scrap it.
Note: Political shift may bring marginalized communities to power that decide their own language should have official status, partly as a sign of respect for the speakers. This lets speakers access state services with ease. But the corpus of such a language may heavily lack in terms of functions the language can serve. Corpus planning manages the expansion of such a language.
